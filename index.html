Whatnot's Whitespace Three structural advantages no competitor can replicate
📺
Live commerce context
Amazon and Shopify are built for async e-commerce. Whatnot owns the live show primitive — viewer counts, bid velocity, chat sentiment, drop timing. No competitor has this data layer, and building it from scratch is a multi-year bet.
🔗
CX + Analytics unified
Sierra handles buyer CX; Copilot handles seller intelligence. Zero competitors combine these surfaces. A seller asking "why are my returns up?" and then "draft an apology message to affected buyers" should flow through one interface — that's our design target.
🤝
Seller-first, not platform-first
Amazon's alerts optimize for Amazon's fee revenue (storage, compliance). Shopify's Sidekick surfaces Shopify Payments data. Whatnot's Copilot is designed around the seller's GMV and take-home — a genuine alignment of incentives competitors can't credibly claim.

What we can learn from competitors

Three patterns are already validated in the wild — we should adopt them, not re-experiment.

Amazon → Lesson
Agentic + approval gates = trust
Amazon's Sep 2025 Seller Assistant proactively flags slow-movers and compliance issues but requires seller sign-off before any action executes. This two-step model — surface the finding, hold on the action — drives adoption without anxiety. Adopt this pattern verbatim for Copilot's agentic V2.
Design implication: Every agentic suggestion needs a clear "approve / dismiss / snooze" affordance. Never auto-execute.
Shopify → Lesson
Direct SQL-layer data access is the right architecture
Sidekick's ShopifyQL integration lets the AI answer arbitrary seller questions by querying live payments and fulfillment data — not a pre-baked dashboard. This is the architecture that makes "which of my shows last month had the best conversion?" answerable. Whatnot's Copilot needs the same structured data API layer, not a set of fixed chart queries.
Design implication: Partner with data infra early. The Copilot experience is only as good as its query surface.
TikTok → Lesson
Advisory-only is a dead end
TikTok Seller Assistant is more capable than initial reports suggested. The official documentation describes it as "agentic AI" that can "reason through problems, plan next steps, and take action on your behalf (with your permission)." It detects the current page in Seller Center and tailors suggestions accordingly — Products, Finance, Orders each get different suggestions. Real actions include updating tracking IDs, appeal tracking, compensation documentation generation, and Shopify sync diagnostics. It also has content tools: "List with AI", AI Fashion Video Maker, Auto-Clipping for livestreams. The page-contextual pattern and approval-gated agentic actions are already live on TikTok — this is a closer competitor than the chatbot framing suggested.
Design implication: Every insight should have a paired "do something about it" action — even if V1 just drafts a message.

02 Design principles

Six rules that resolve most design questions before they reach a debate. When in doubt, pick the principle.

01

Answers worth returning to

The conversation is chat. The answer is a durable artifact — pinnable, schedulable, shareable. Both exist; the goal is that what the bot produces has a life beyond the thread it came from.

02

Streaming over spinners

Show partial data the moment it arrives. Name each step in seller language. No generic "loading" — sellers should always know what's happening and why it's slow.

03

Proactive by default

The bot opens with insights worth a seller's attention, not a blank "ask anything." It earns its place by showing value before it's asked.

04

Show your work

Every number cites its source, row count, freshness, and method. Sellers will make revenue decisions on these answers — they need to be able to verify.

05

Threads are analyses

Threads aren't chat history. They're saved workspaces — pinnable, schedulable, shareable, comment-able. A thread is the smallest unit of repeatable work.

06

Honest about uncertainty

Confidence is labeled. "I don't know" is a valid answer. Low-data answers are flagged. The bot never fabricates a number to seem helpful.

03 Current state critique

Two AI products being built in parallel, serving different use cases. The challenge: make sure they converge, not diverge.

Now — two products in parallel
Whatnot AI Copilot ← this doc
In development. Answers analytics and business questions. Launching soon as a standalone seller tool.
+
Sierra (CX Bot)
Also in development. Handles buyer tickets and support flows. Separate surface, separate context, no analytics awareness.
→
Future — one unified interface
Whatnot AI Copilot
One consistent entry point, everywhere in the app. Tool calls route to the right capability — analytics, CX, inventory, scheduling — transparently. The seller never thinks about which bot to use.

Parallel ≠ unified

Building two products at once is the right short-term call. The risk is they ship and stay separate forever — two entry points, two contexts, two mental models for the seller.

No shared context

Whatnot AI Copilot has no awareness of open CX tickets. Sierra has no awareness of a seller's GMV or show performance. Each starts from zero — the seller's full context is never available in one place.

Siloed actions

Neither product can act across domains today. Analytics can't draft a buyer response. Sierra can't surface GMV trends. Tool calls and a shared backend are the bridge.

Convergence risk

The longer two separate products run independently, the harder they are to merge — separate teams, separate infra, separate UX patterns. The time to align on a unified vision is now, before each calcifies.

Empty state

Opens to "Ask about your business" — a chat product, not an analytics product. No data, no insights, no signal of what's possible.

No persistence

Navigate away and the question vanishes. Questions must be durable objects from the moment of submission, not transient UI state.

Ship exactly two — not six
These are options to choose from, not a checklist. v1 ships two entry points, each doing a different job:

1 · Left-nav "Ask Whatnot" (B)

Plain text responses that explain, reason, confirm, and converse. Powers analytics explanations today and every agentic interaction tomorrow — step confirmations, clarification requests, approval gates, action summaries.

Tuesday's show outperformed Friday's for three reasons:

2. Inventory mix. You listed 18 Pokémon cards on Tuesday vs 6 on Friday. Your buyer base has a 2.4× higher conversion rate on Pokémon vs other categories.

3. Viewer duration. Average watch time on Tuesday was 22 minutes vs 11 minutes on Friday, which correlates with higher per-session spend in your store.

What it takes — ingesting Whatnot's support content
A retrieval pipeline (RAG) over official content: crawl Help Center, policy pages & Seller University · transcribe support videos (speech-to-text, segmented by timestamp) · chunk, embed, and store with source metadata · on a question, hybrid-search → rerank → answer only from retrieved passages, with citations. Knowledge answers are the single biggest deflection lever — lower-lift than analytics SQL or agentic actions, and the same capability Sierra needs for CX. This is where the two products converge.

16 Risks & open questions

The things that could kill this product, and what we'd do about each.

Hallucinated numbers destroy trust

A bot that confidently states the wrong GMV once will not be trusted again. The damage is asymmetric — one bad answer outweighs a hundred good ones.

Mitigation: Every numeric answer cites source, row count, query. Adversarial QA on the top 100 question types. Low-confidence labeling.

Slow queries kill adoption

If 30% of questions take >10s, sellers stop asking. The current bot already has this problem — sellers walk away mid-query.

Mitigation: Aggressive pre-computation of common questions. Streaming partial results. Long-query backgrounding with notification.

Privacy & data scope

Sellers will ask "show me my top buyers by name." How much PII is safe to surface? What about cross-show buyer analysis that exposes patterns?

Mitigation: PII access audit-logged. Buyer-level data gated behind seller permission. Aggregate-only for cross-show category questions.

Mobile is where shows happen

Most Whatnot sellers stream from phones or tablets. If the bot is desktop-only or feels degraded on mobile, half the audience is left out.

Mitigation: Mobile-first design for v1 chat surface. Voice input. Push for scheduled alerts. Responsive answer cards.

Cost per question

LLM inference + query execution costs scale with usage. A power seller asking 50 questions a day at $0.10 each is $5/day, $1,500/year in pure cost.

Mitigation: Tiered model (cheap model for routing, expensive for analysis). Query result caching. Rate limits at the extreme tail.

"Just another chatbot" perception

Sellers are saturated with AI chat features. If our bot looks like Intercom, ChatGPT, or Sidekick, it gets ignored despite being more powerful.

Mitigation: Lead with proactive insights, not chat. Brand the surface — "Ask Whatnot" not "AI Assistant." Show data, not bubbles.

Question diversity is unbounded

Sellers will ask things the system can't handle. Every "I don't know" is an adoption risk. But over-reaching produces wrong answers (see risk #1).

Mitigation: Conservative routing — refuse confidently, suggest closest answerable. Telemetry on failed questions to expand coverage.

Two surfaces, two experiences

If the side panel and the full-page thread workspace drift apart, sellers learn two different products. Inconsistency kills trust.

Mitigation: Side panel is literally the thread workspace at narrower width. Same components, same actions, same composer.
Open questions still to resolve
1. Should the bot have a name (e.g. "Hubie") or stay "Ask Whatnot"? Named bots build affinity but also expectations of personality we may not want to fulfill.
2. How aggressive should proactive insights be? Daily can fatigue; weekly may miss urgent things. Per-seller cadence learning?
3. Pricing — included in seller tier, or a paid power feature? Bundled increases adoption; paid covers cost.
4. When do we open this up to data the seller doesn't own (category benchmarks)? Big trust unlock but big privacy lift for buyers.
